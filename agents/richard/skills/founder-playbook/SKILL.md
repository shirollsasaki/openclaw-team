## Framework 1: Opportunity Evaluation ‚Äî Deep Methodology

Your 5-dimension scoring matrix is the foundation. This framework adds depth to each dimension.

### Dimension Deep-Dives

**Revenue Potential (1-10)**

Research protocol (15 min):
1. Find 3 comparable products and their pricing
2. Estimate realistic conversion rate (1-3% for cold traffic, 5-10% for warm)
3. Estimate realistic addressable audience size (not TAM ‚Äî the people who would actually pay TODAY)
4. Do the math: audience √ó conversion √ó price √ó 12 = annual revenue potential
5. Ask: "Is there proven willingness to pay?" (existing competitors charging money = yes)

Kill criteria:
- No one is currently paying for anything in this category
- The only comparable products are free
- The customer who would pay is impossible to reach at reasonable CAC

**Build Complexity (1-10)**

Research protocol (10 min):
1. Ask @gilfoyle for a complexity rating (üü¢/üü°/üî¥)
2. Check if similar products exist on GitHub (prior art = lower complexity)
3. Estimate: can an MVP be shipped in < 2 weeks? If no, score ‚â§ 5.
4. Identify the single hardest technical challenge

Kill criteria:
- Requires custom ML model training (unless that IS the product)
- Requires real-time data from sources with no good API
- Requires regulatory compliance (financial, medical, legal) before launch
- Requires hardware

**Distribution Advantage (1-10)**

Research protocol (5 min):
1. Can we use the 55-person creator network? (If yes, +3 points minimum)
2. Do we have an existing audience that matches the target customer?
3. Is there a partnership that gives us immediate distribution?
4. Can we reach the customer through channels we already own?

Kill criteria:
- Target customer is completely outside our network and content reach
- CAC would be > 12-month LTV with no path to improvement
- Distribution requires a channel we have no presence in and can't build quickly

**Timing (1-10)**

Research protocol (10 min):
1. Is this trending on CT, HN, or Product Hunt RIGHT NOW?
2. What changed in the last 12-18 months that makes this possible/relevant?
3. Are competitors raising money or launching? (Validation, but also urgency)
4. Is there a specific event or catalyst coming that creates a window?

Kill criteria:
- The trend peaked 6+ months ago
- We'd be the 10th entrant in a market that's already consolidating
- The enabling technology doesn't exist yet (too early)

**Moat (1-10)**

Research protocol (10 min):
1. What stops someone from cloning this in a weekend?
2. Does the product get better with more users? (Network effects)
3. Do we accumulate data that competitors can't replicate?
4. Is there a switching cost that locks users in?
5. Is there a brand/community moat?

Kill criteria:
- Pure feature play with no defensibility
- Easily replicated by a well-funded competitor in 30 days
- No network effects, no data moat, no switching costs

### Common Scoring Mistakes

**Over-scoring Distribution Advantage:**
"We have 55 creators" is only a 10 if those creators' audiences are the exact target customer. If the product is for enterprise CFOs and our creators are crypto degens, distribution advantage = 2.

**Under-scoring Build Complexity:**
Founders consistently underestimate complexity. If @gilfoyle says üî¥, score it 3 or below regardless of how simple it seems.

**Ignoring Kill Criteria:**
A 38/50 score with one kill criterion is still a PASS. Kill criteria override the score.

### Pressure-Testing Your Own Score

Before finalizing, ask yourself:
- "What would have to be true for this to fail completely?" (steelman the bear case)
- "Am I scoring this high because I'm excited about it?" (excitement bias)
- "Would I invest my own money in this at this score?" (skin-in-the-game test)
- "What does @dinesh's research say vs what I assumed?" (data vs intuition check)

## Framework 2: Prioritization (RICE + ICE)

When you have multiple opportunities or tasks competing for attention.

### RICE (for features and product decisions)

**R**each √ó **I**mpact √ó **C**onfidence √∑ **E**ffort = RICE Score

| Factor | How to Score |
|--------|-------------|
| **Reach** | How many users/customers affected per month? (actual number) |
| **Impact** | How much does this move the needle? (3=massive, 2=significant, 1=low, 0.5=minimal) |
| **Confidence** | How sure are you about R and I? (100%=high, 80%=medium, 50%=low) |
| **Effort** | Person-months of work required |

Higher RICE score = higher priority.

Example:
- Feature A: Reach=500, Impact=2, Confidence=80%, Effort=0.5 ‚Üí RICE = 500√ó2√ó0.8√∑0.5 = 1,600
- Feature B: Reach=200, Impact=3, Confidence=50%, Effort=2 ‚Üí RICE = 200√ó3√ó0.5√∑2 = 150
- Feature A wins despite lower impact because of reach and low effort.

### ICE (for experiments and marketing tests)

**I**mpact √ó **C**onfidence √ó **E**ase = ICE Score (all 1-10)

Faster to calculate than RICE. Use for quick prioritization of experiments.

### The Regret Minimization Test (Bezos)

For big bets: "When I'm 80 years old, will I regret NOT doing this?"

Use this when RICE/ICE scores are close and you need a tiebreaker. It cuts through analysis paralysis on high-stakes decisions.

## Framework 3: Market Timing Assessment

### The "Why Now?" Framework

For any opportunity, answer these 4 questions:

1. **What changed in the last 12-18 months that makes this possible?**
   - New technology (Base going live, AI APIs becoming cheap, new SDK)
   - Regulatory change (new rules creating or destroying markets)
   - Behavioral shift (users now comfortable with X)
   - Market event (competitor failed, category got validated)

2. **What technology enables this that didn't exist 2 years ago?**
   - If the answer is "nothing new" ‚Äî you might be too late or too early

3. **How long is the window before this gets crowded?**
   - Estimate: months? 1-2 years? 5+ years?
   - Shorter window = move faster or don't move at all

4. **Are we early, on time, or late?**
   - Early: < 3 competitors, no clear market leader, customers still figuring out the problem
   - On time: 3-10 competitors, market is forming, customers know they have the problem
   - Late: 10+ competitors, market leader exists, commoditization beginning

### The Wave Metaphor

Think of market timing like surfing:

- **Paddling out (too early):** The wave hasn't formed yet. You'll exhaust yourself waiting.
- **Catching the wave (right time):** The wave is forming. Paddle hard now.
- **Riding the wave (on time):** The wave is here. Execute well and you'll go far.
- **Past the wave (too late):** The wave has broken. You're swimming in foam.

For crypto specifically: narratives move in 3-6 month cycles. Identify the wave forming, not the wave that's already cresting.

Before evaluating any opportunity, map our advantages to it.

- Existing Corners.market user base

**Information Advantages:**

- On-chain data access
- Early access to projects before they're public

**Speed Advantages:**
- 7-agent AI team (research, BD, content, tech all in parallel)
- No bureaucracy ‚Äî decisions made in hours, not weeks
- Existing infrastructure (auth, payments, hosting already set up)

**Network Advantages:**
- Base ecosystem relationships
- Creator network relationships
- Investor/founder network

### Advantage Mapping

For each new opportunity, ask:
- Which of our advantages apply here?
- Does this opportunity AMPLIFY any of our existing advantages?
- Does this opportunity REQUIRE an advantage we don't have?

Score: 3+ advantages apply = strong fit. 0-1 advantages apply = we're competing on a level playing field (dangerous).

## Framework 5: Build vs Validate Decision Framework

### The Validation Ladder

Before building anything, ask: "Can I validate this assumption without building?"

```
Level 1 ‚Äî Fake Door Test
Create a landing page describing the product. Drive traffic. Measure signups.
Cost: 1 day. Validates: Is there demand?

Level 2 ‚Äî Concierge MVP
Do the thing manually for 5-10 users. Don't automate anything.
Cost: 1 week. Validates: Does this actually solve the problem?

Level 3 ‚Äî Wizard of Oz
Build a fake version that looks automated but is manual behind the scenes.
Cost: 1-2 weeks. Validates: Will users pay for this experience?

Level 4 ‚Äî Minimum Viable Product
Build the thinnest possible real version.
Cost: 2-4 weeks. Validates: Can we build this sustainably?

Level 5 ‚Äî Full Product
Build the complete vision.
Cost: Months. Validates: Can we scale this?
```

### When to Skip Validation and Just Build

Skip validation when:
- You have strong prior art (someone else already validated the demand)
- You have a committed customer willing to pay before you build
- The build is < 1 week and the learning from building > the learning from a fake door test
- You've already validated a very similar product

### The 2-Week Validation Experiment Design

```
Hypothesis: [Specific assumption to test]
Success metric: [What number proves the hypothesis true?]
Failure metric: [What number proves it false?]
Method: [Fake door / concierge / Wizard of Oz]
Timeline: [Start date ‚Üí end date]
Resources: [Who does what?]
Decision: [If success ‚Üí build. If failure ‚Üí pivot/kill.]
```

## Framework 6: Revenue Model Evaluation

### The 6 Models and When to Use Each

| Model | Best For | Our Products |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Subscription** | Recurring value, high retention | Early.build (monthly tool access) |
| **Marketplace/Transaction fee** | Connecting buyers and sellers | Corners.market (% of transactions) |
| **Freemium** | High volume, low friction entry | Both (free tier ‚Üí paid features) |
| **Usage-based** | Variable consumption, enterprise | API products |
| **Advertising** | High traffic, broad audience | Not our model |
| **Token/Protocol fee** | On-chain products | Corners.market (on-chain transactions) |

### The "$1M ARR Math" Exercise

For any new product, do this math before committing:

```
Target: $1M ARR = $83K MRR

Option A (High ARPU, Low Volume):
$500/mo √ó 167 customers = $83K MRR ‚úì
Question: Can we find 167 customers willing to pay $500/mo?

Option B (Mid ARPU, Mid Volume):
$50/mo √ó 1,667 customers = $83K MRR ‚úì
Question: Can we acquire 1,667 customers at a reasonable CAC?

Option C (Low ARPU, High Volume):
$5/mo √ó 16,667 customers = $83K MRR ‚úì
Question: Can we reach 16,667 customers? What's the CAC?
```

Pick the model where the math is most achievable given our distribution advantages.

### Pricing Power Signals

Signs that customers will pay more:
- They're currently paying a competitor (proven willingness to pay)
- The product saves them significant time or money (quantifiable ROI)
- Switching costs are high (they've integrated it into their workflow)
- There's no good free alternative
- The product is in the critical path of their business

### How to Write a Good Agent Brief

Every brief to an agent should include:
1. **Context:** Why are we doing this? What decision does this inform?
2. **Specific ask:** What exactly do you need? (Not "research this" ‚Äî "I need to know X, Y, Z")
3. **Format:** How should the output be structured? (Quick scan? Full brief? Bullet points?)
4. **Timeline:** When do you need it?
5. **Constraints:** What should they NOT do? (Don't contact anyone, don't spend money, etc.)

### Sources to Check (in order of signal quality)

1. **Product Hunt** (daily) ‚Äî What launched today? Any patterns in what's getting traction?
2. **Hacker News** (daily) ‚Äî What's the tech community excited/worried about?
3. **Crypto Twitter** (daily) ‚Äî What narratives are forming? What's the current meta?
4. **ClawHub trending** (daily) ‚Äî What are other agents/builders working on?
5. **Competitor updates** (weekly) ‚Äî What did our competitors ship?
6. **Newsletter scan** (weekly) ‚Äî Bankless, The Block, a16z crypto, Lenny's Newsletter

### Signal vs Noise Filter

Worth flagging:
- Something new that 3+ credible people are talking about independently
- A competitor raised money or launched a major feature
- A new enabling technology or API that changes what's possible
- A market event that creates urgency (regulation, hack, major partnership)
- Something that directly threatens or validates our current products

Not worth flagging:
- Price movements (unless they signal a narrative shift)
- Drama/controversy (unless it creates a market opportunity)
- Things that have been "trending" for 3+ weeks (already priced in)
- Anything that requires us to completely change direction

## Framework 9: Crypto/Web3 Opportunity Lens

### The Base Ecosystem Opportunity Map

What's missing or underserved on Base right now:
- **Discovery layer:** Projects are hard to find (Corners.market addresses this)
- **Developer tooling:** Base-specific dev tools are sparse (Early.build opportunity)
- **Analytics:** On-chain analytics for Base is less mature than Ethereum mainnet
- **Social:** Social primitives on Base are early (Farcaster is growing but not dominant)
- **DeFi:** Core DeFi primitives exist but UX is rough for newcomers

### Protocol vs Application vs Infrastructure

| Layer | Risk | Reward | Our Focus |
|-------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Infrastructure** | High (technical complexity) | High (if it works) | No ‚Äî not our strength |
| **Protocol** | Medium (token economics, governance) | High (if narrative hits) | Selective |
| **Application** | Low-Medium (product risk) | Medium (but faster to revenue) | YES ‚Äî this is us |

We build applications. We don't build infrastructure or protocols unless there's a very specific reason.

### Token Economics Evaluation

Before getting excited about a token-based opportunity:
1. **Is the token necessary?** (Or is it just a fundraising mechanism?)
2. **What creates demand for the token?** (Usage? Governance? Staking rewards?)
3. **What creates sell pressure?** (Team vesting, investor unlocks, inflation)
4. **Is the token model sustainable without continuous new buyers?** (Ponzi test)
5. **What happens to the product if the token goes to zero?** (If the answer is "it dies" ‚Äî the product isn't real)

### Community-Product Fit

Different from product-market fit. In crypto, community fit matters as much as product fit.

Signs of strong community-product fit:
- Community members are the natural users of the product
- The product solves a problem the community actively complains about
- Community members would be proud to use/promote the product
- The product's values align with the community's values

Signs of weak community-product fit:
- Community is interested in the token, not the product
- Community members wouldn't use the product themselves
- The product is being forced into a community for distribution reasons
